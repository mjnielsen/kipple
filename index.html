Kipple

A diceware passphrase generator for Mac OS X.

What is Kipple?

Kipple is a diceware passphrase generator. It randomly selects a specified number of words from a list of 7,776 commonly-used words. It can also throw a symbol into one of the words at random, if you think you can handle that much excitement.

Kipple is also a word invented by Philip K. Dick is his novel Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?. In the novel, kipple is the rubbish and detritus that builds up when humans are no longer present, a concept know as entropy in thermodynamics. Entropy is also an important concept in information theory and is used to quantify the strength of passwords or passphrases.

How do I use it?

Just set the number of words you'd like in your passphrase, and check the box to include an additional character if you want. The rest is taken care of by Kipple.

If you can't reliably commit the passphrase to memory immediately (I know I can't), write it down on a piece of paper that you can refer to if necessary. Keep that piece of paper safe. Once you've memorised the the passphrase, burn the paper and flush the ashes down the toilet.

Remember: any security system is only as strong as its weakest link. A strong passphrase is only useful if it is kept a secret. Never tell anybody all your passphrase. Seriously. If you ever suspect that anyone else knows your passphrase, change it immediately.

It's best to generate your passphrase in a private place where no one else can see what you're doing. In case that's not possible, Kipple hides the generated passphrase by default. Use the unhide button to show the passphrase only for as long as you need to.


Why diceware?

If you try to come up with a password or phrase that's easy to remember on your own, it will probably be short. It will also be predictable—surprisingly so for anyone who knows even a little about you.

That's where diceware comes in. Diceware started out as a more-convenient method of generating passphrases using a hardware random number generator: dice.

By using the rolls of several dice to select a word at random from a list, it becomes much more difficult for an adversary—a criminal, or even the government*— to guess your password.

Since the passphrase is made up of a sequence of words, rather than letters, numbers or symbols, it is much more difficult to attack via brute force. Consider this: even if create a 10 letter password using all of the available letters, symbols and numbers on your keyboard (of which there are approximately 100), the number of possible combinations of those letters, symbols and number is 10010—that's a 1 with 20 zeroes after it. On the other hand, if you use just a six word diceware passphrase, using words selected from the list of 7,776, there are 7,7766 possible combinations of those words— that's approximately 2 with 23 zeroes after it.

Which do you think is easier to remember? A string of 10 random characters, or a string of six words?

Kipple uses the same list of words that is used in the manual diceware method to generate a random passphrase for you, with the added bonus of not having to actually roll dice.
